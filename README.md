*_Carving ‚ÄúFat‚Äù Sine Waves from Noise

Traditionally, a pure sine wave is generated by tracing a mathematical function, producing a single precise frequency. In the Fishtail approach, however, we ‚Äúcarve‚Äù a sine wave out of noise rather than tracing it. This means starting with broad-spectrum noise and filtering it down to a narrow band centered on the desired pitch. The resulting tone is still sinusoidal in character but not perfectly pure ‚Äì it has a slight frequency width (a ‚Äúfatness‚Äù). In practice, the carved tone might occupy a bandwidth on the order of 1/12 of a semitone around the target frequency (for example, a few Hz of spread around an A‚ÇÑ pitch). The amplitude is highest at the center frequency and tapers off toward the edges, somewhat like a teardrop shape in the frequency domain. This technique is essentially a form of subtractive synthesis: by applying a very sharp band-pass filter to white noise, one can ‚Äúemphasize a frequency band‚Äù and literally transform noise into a pitched tone Ôøº. SuperCollider‚Äôs Resonz filter, for instance, can be used in this way, taking white noise and carving out a near-sine wave at the desired frequency Ôøº. The outcome is a sine wave that has been sculpted from quantum-like randomness rather than computed directly ‚Äì an artful touch that introduces subtle organic qualities.

Why carve instead of trace? The answer lies in psychoacoustics and musical aesthetics. A perfectly pure sine wave can sound thin or sterile, whereas a slightly ‚Äúfat‚Äù sine has a richer character. Fatness in sound synthesis often refers to the effect of many closely tuned frequencies reinforcing each other, as in an ensemble. When multiple oscillators or instruments play the same note with tiny differences in tuning, each harmonic isn‚Äôt a single spike but a cluster of frequencies ‚Äì yielding a broader spectral peak (see figures below). Our ear perceives such a tone as fuller and louder, because it integrates energy across that narrow band of frequencies Ôøº. In contrast, a single perfect oscillator produces razor-thin spectral lines, which can sound comparatively weak.

Figure: Spectrum of a fat sound (ensemble or modulated oscillator). Each harmonic has spread into a wider band of frequencies rather than a single spike Ôøº. Slight detuning between oscillators (or low-frequency modulation of one oscillator) causes the individual partials to ‚Äúsmear‚Äù into narrow clusters Ôøº. The overall effect is a rounder, more robust tone Ôøº. In analog synths, random drift (from temperature, supply voltage, etc.) naturally introduces a few Hertz of fluctuation, spreading each harmonic and ‚Äúcreating the ‚Äòfat sound‚Äô phenomenon‚Äù Ôøº.

Carving a sine wave from noise inherently produces this desirable bandwidth. Even with a very narrow filter, there are microscopic fluctuations as the noise excites the filter ‚Äì the sine ‚Äúwobbles‚Äù ever so slightly within a ¬±Œµ Hz range. Listeners may not consciously detect the micro-modulation, but they feel the added warmth and presence. It‚Äôs a form of psychoacoustic trickery: the carved wave retains the pure pitch at its core, but the fuzzy edges give it life. In practical terms, a carved sine can achieve greater perceived loudness and fullness at the same amplitude than an ultra-precise sine, because the ear‚Äôs frequency-domain integration treats those extra sideband energies as part of the sound‚Äôs body Ôøº. In essence, Fishtail marries science and art ‚Äì using a signal-processing technique (high-Q filtering of noise) to achieve an artistic outcome (a more engaging sound). Some synthesists ‚Äútrace‚Äù their waveforms with perfect oscillators, but here we ‚Äúcarve‚Äù them ‚Äì literally sculpting order (the musical tone) out of chaos (broadband noise). It‚Äôs a distinctive approach that listeners are likely to find special and appealing.

The Ratio-Based Pitch Lattice

At the heart of Fishtail is a ratio-based pitch lattice ‚Äì a two-dimensional grid of tuning intervals constructed from a given base frequency. Instead of the fixed 12 equally spaced semitones per octave, this lattice uses specific interval generators to produce a rich palette of pitches. Formally, if we denote the base frequency as f0, and choose two generator intervals (multipliers) ‚Äì one rational (let‚Äôs call it r) and one irrational (for example the golden ratio œÜ ‚âà 1.618) ‚Äì then every lattice pitch can be expressed as:

f_{i,j} = f_0 \cdot r^i \cdot \phi^j,

for some integers i, j (which can be positive, negative, or zero) Ôøº. In Amy‚Äôs concept for Fishtail, r might be a simple just-intonation ratio like 3/2 (a perfect fifth), and œÜ provides an irrational step to fill in the gaps between purely rational tunings Ôøº. Using these two generators, the lattice spans an incredibly fine-grained set of pitches. Every step up by i increases frequency by a fifth (3:2), and every step by j multiplies by œÜ (‚âà 833.09 cents if taken as a direct interval). Because no finite combination of 3/2 intervals will exactly equal a œÜ step (rational vs irrational independence), the lattice never wraps around or repeats redundantly Ôøº ‚Äì it produces a quasi-continuous spectrum of pitches. In fact, with reasonable bounds on i and j, the lattice points densely cover the frequency range. Even a relatively small 2D grid (say i,j from ‚Äì5 to +5) yields many unique pitches; a larger 128√ó128 grid would create a near-continuum where adjacent points differ by only a few cents or less Ôøº. In other words, this system can approximate or directly produce virtually any tuning one might desire, going far beyond the 12 discrete tones of the conventional scale.

Notably, Fishtail does not enforce octave equivalence unless explicitly desired. Unlike standard scales where pitches are folded into a single octave (class equivalence mod 2:1), here each lattice point is an absolute frequency. One could introduce the octave (2/1) as another independent ratio if needed, but by default an increase of +i=12 fifths, for example, is not reduced mod octave ‚Äì it‚Äôs just a higher frequency Ôøº. This approach is intentional: it preserves the precise frequency relationships for use in the tempo lattice (discussed next), and it underscores the idea that pitch is a continuum. Musically, it allows progressions that weave through different registers without being forced into repetition by octave reduction. It‚Äôs a step toward breaking the 128-note, 12-semitone grid that people are used to ‚Äì expanding their perspective to see pitch as a limitless field. However, this is done gently: the system can still map these new frequencies onto the familiar MIDI note numbers (0‚Äì127) so that existing keyboards and sequencers can interface with it. Locking the Tempo Lattice to Pitch

Perhaps the most innovative aspect of Fishtail is how it links tempo (rhythm) to the same ratio lattice as pitch. Just as we quantize pitches to specific frequency ratios, we also quantize tempo values to be in simple relationship with the base frequency f0. The idea is to avoid arbitrary tempos that clash with the tuning; instead, choose tempi where the waveform of f0 fits an integer number of cycles in each beat or measure Ôøº. In practical terms, this means the beats are synchronized with the zero-crossings of the base tone ‚Äì eliminating phasing or clicking that could occur when a note‚Äôs phase and the rhythmic grid are at odds Ôøº. Mathematically, if we consider a measure of B beats at tempo T (in BPM), the condition for alignment is:

f_0 \times \text{(measure duration)} = f_0 \times \frac{B \cdot 60}{T} \; \in \; \mathbb{Z},

i.e. f0 times the measure length (in seconds) should equal an integer. This yields allowable tempi of the form

T = \frac{B \cdot 60 \cdot f_0}{k},

for some integer k (the number of cycles of f0 per measure) Ôøº. All such T will ensure the fundamental pitch is perfectly phase-locked to the beat grid. For example, with f0=440 Hz and a 4-beat measure (B=4, common 4/4 time), $4 \cdot 60 \cdot 440 = 105600$. Dividing by k gives a family of BPM values: k=800 yields 132 BPM, k=1650 gives 64 BPM, k=400 gives 264 BPM, k=3300 gives 32 BPM, and so on Ôøº. These are precisely the tempos cited as ‚Äúmathematically correlating with the key of A‚Äì440‚Äù in the concept ‚Äì they have a clear rational link to the pitch. If we retune the base frequency, the whole set of allowed tempos shifts accordingly Ôøº. For instance, if f0 is changed to 420 Hz, the formula yields 126 BPM instead of 132 BPM as the first related tempo (since $4 \cdot 60 \cdot 420 / 800 = 126$) Ôøº. In general, a lower base pitch results in slightly lower quantized tempi for equivalent k, and vice versa ‚Äì the tempo lattice is effectively key-dependent.

We can think of this as a tempo lattice analogous to the pitch lattice. In fact, tempo ratios end up being drawn from the same set of rational ratios that define the tuning intervals Ôøº. By choosing integer values of k (cycles per measure) intelligently ‚Äì for example, using the least common multiple of denominators of our pitch ratios as a starting point ‚Äì we ensure that every note in the scale aligns with the beat grid. (If a certain pitch is $f_0 \cdot \frac{p}{q}$, and we choose k as a multiple of q, then that pitch will complete $p$ cycles in $\frac{p}{q}$ of the base pitch‚Äôs cycle count, which fits into an integer number of beats by construction„Äê15‚Ä†„Äë.) In implementation terms, once the pitch lattice is set, one can similarly derive a list or table of allowed BPM values by scanning through integer k values Ôøº. The result is an infinite yet structured set of tempi ‚Äì a ‚Äúrhythm scale‚Äù that complements the pitch scale. Musically, this opens up fascinating possibilities: one could modulate the tempo to a new value that is harmonically related to the old tempo because both are tied to the underlying f0. It‚Äôs a step toward treating tempo as a musical pitch, subject to transposition and modulation in a way that sounds natural to the ear because the ear is hearing consistent phase relationships.

It‚Äôs important to note that while we use an irrational interval (œÜ) on the pitch side to enrich the tuning, we do not use irrational ratios for tempo in this design Ôøº. The golden ratio in pitch ensures a never-repeating scale; but a golden ratio in time (e.g. a tempo related by œÜ) would mean beats that never realign neatly ‚Äì essentially a constantly shifting phase that never resolves. That might be interesting for experimental polyrhythms or phasing pieces, but for the core concept here we keep tempo relationships rational (integer cycle counts) so that the rhythmic structure periodically syncs up. The tempo lattice, therefore, is built from rational divisions of the base tempo (just as a traditional musician might subdivide a beat into 2,3,4 etc., here we subdivide the base measure into k cycles of f0). The inclusion of œÜ in the pitch lattice already provides an extremely fine pitch resolution, so the lack of œÜ in tempo still leaves us with a dense set of tempi ‚Äì and crucially, ones that are periodically compatible with the tones being played Ôøº.

By locking the tempo to the pitch lattice, we are, in a sense, extending the idea of a ‚Äúscale.‚Äù In conventional Western music, a scale quantizes pitch (12-tone equal temperament being the dominant example) while tempo is chosen rather freely (often in round numbers or conventional ranges like 120 BPM for dance, etc.). Conclusion and Outlook

The Fishtail project represents a fusion of artistic intuition and scientific rigor.
